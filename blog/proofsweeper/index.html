Proofsweeper

2021-05-22


When we want to show that something is true, we say that we want to prove it. This word is not uncommon. For instance, we say that the accused are proven guilty or innocent in trials.

In Mathematics, proving something means showing that it must necessarily be true. In essence, a mathematician's job consists of proving statements. Lawyers, scholars, and other scientists do this too, but their methods differ from those of mathematicians in their very nature. Scientists use real-world data to try to support their reasonings and help understand patterns. Mathematicians do no such thing: their proofs are purely logical. Thus, learning how to read and write proofs is an essential part of a mathematician's initial training.

In this post, I want to briefly showcase the basic tools used by mathematicians for building logically sound proofs.

And to do that, we'll play Minesweeper.

Types of proof

Mathematical statements usually take the form of deductive statements: they have a premise, or hypothesis, which we suppose, know, or assume to be true, and our mission is to reach, from them, a conclusion. We can represent this idea with the following notation:

$p \implies q$
